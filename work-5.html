Hydraulic Transients

Analysis & Accumulator Design

This project, developed as part of my master's thesis, focused on mitigating hydraulic transients in steelmaking equipment through the use of piston-type pressure accumulators. These pressure spikes were causing recurring oil leaks and mechanical failures in a high-pressure circuit.

A simplified hydraulic circuit was designed and modeled in Automation Studio. It aimed to replicate the behavior of actuators, pumps, and valves during transient events to evaluate the accumulator‚Äôs effectiveness.

To control the hydraulic transients, a piston-type accumulator was selected for its fast response time and precise pressure regulation capabilities.

The accumulator was dimensioned with a 60 liter nominal volume and optimized pre-charge pressure to match the operating conditions of the steelmaking system.

This type ensures effective energy storage and release, minimizing pressure spikes without introducing significant delays, making it ideal for protecting actuators under frequent cycling loads.

The simulation showed a clear difference between systems with and without the accumulator. Pressure spikes were significantly reduced, and flow profiles became smoother. These results highlight the accumulator's effectiveness in absorbing energy surges.

This improvement reduces mechanical wear, improves system safety, and extends component life‚Äîmaking the solution ideal for industrial steel applications with frequent actuation cycles.

This final phase strengthened my understanding of transient suppression techniques and accumulator sizing. The project bridged simulation with real-world behavior and emphasized the importance of energy storage in hydraulic circuit stability.

Tools like Automation Studio and validation through real pressure and flow data were key in proving the design‚Äôs impact and reliability. This work reflects my ability to deliver data-driven solutions to complex industrial challenges.
